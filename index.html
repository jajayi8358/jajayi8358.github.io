Ten Interesting Facts About Flowers

Read time: 5 minutes

Flowers beguile us with their lovely scent and striking beauty, but many flowers have hidden attributes. From unexpected relatives to ancient rituals, here are 10 fascinating facts about flowers.

  1. Roses are related to fruit: Roses, one of the world’s most cherished flowers, are surprisingly related to apples, raspberries, cherries, peaches, and almonds.
  2. Tulip bulbs once rivaled gold: In 17th century Holland, tulip bulbs were more valuable than gold during the infamous “Tulip Mania.”
  3. Aster leaves warded off evil spirits: Ancient civilizations believed that burning aster leaves could keep evil spirits at bay.
  4. Tulip bulbs as food: During times of desperation, such as World War II, tulip bulbs were used as a substitute for onions in recipes.
  5. Chrysanthemums and funerals: In Malta, chrysanthemums are closely associated with funerals and are considered unlucky.
  6. Saffron's floral origins: The luxurious and expensive spice saffron is harvested from the stigmas of crocus flowers.
  7. The largest flower smells of decay: The titan arum, also known as the corpse flower, produces the world’s largest bloom, standing 10 feet high and 3 feet wide. It smells like decaying flesh.
  8. California's flower power: Almost 60 percent of fresh-cut flowers in the U.S. are grown in California.
  9. Scotland's wild thistle defense: The national flower of Scotland, the wild thistle, earned its title after helping the Scots escape Viking invaders.
  10. The sacred lotus: Ancient Egyptians revered the lotus as a symbol of resurrection and eternal life. It was commonly used in burial rituals.

Exploring Unique Flower Facts

Read time: 5 minutes

Flowers have more to offer than just beauty. They play significant roles in history, culture, and even science. Here are some additional intriguing facts:

  1. Roses are related to fruit: Roses, beyond their beauty, share a surprising relationship with fruits like apples and peaches.
  2. Tulip bulbs once rivaled gold: Tulip Mania in the 17th century had tulip bulbs valued more than gold in the Netherlands.
  3. Aster leaves warded off evil spirits: In ancient times, people burned aster leaves to drive away evil spirits.
  4. Chrysanthemums are symbols of death: In many European countries, chrysanthemums are considered symbols of death and are used for funerals.
  5. Saffron’s floral source: The spice saffron is derived from the flower Crocus sativus, and it takes a huge number of flowers to produce just a small amount of the spice.
  6. Flowers can help with anxiety: Lavender flowers are known to have a calming effect, often used in aromatherapy for reducing anxiety and stress.
  7. The corpse flower's stench: The titan arum, or corpse flower, produces a massive bloom with a distinct smell of rotting flesh.
  8. California dominates flower farming: Around 60% of all fresh-cut flowers in the United States are grown in California.
  9. Thistles and Viking invasions: The thistle, Scotland’s national flower, allegedly helped warn Scots of an impending Viking invasion.
  10. The sacred lotus: The ancient Egyptians held the lotus in high regard, associating it with rebirth and eternal life.
